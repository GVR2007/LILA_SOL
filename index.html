Dual Water Sources

The system draws from two complementary sources, providing year-round water reliability across both monsoon and dry seasons.

🔀 Source Selection by Season

🏞️

Oorani Pond

Used during and after monsoon (2 months rain + recovery). Large storage capacity. Main source. Needs annual desilting to reduce bottom organics and odor.

🪣

Small Well

Used during long dry months (up to 10 months). Lower turbidity, more stable TDS (~737 mg/L). Reliable even in peak summer when pond dries.

Step-by-Step Treatment Flow

Water moves through 9 natural stages from raw source to clean tap — each designed for simple village-level operation with locally available materials.

🌊 Water Journey: Source → Tap
💧
Stage 1 · Intake
Oorani Pond / Small Well
Raw water from the source. Pond water may carry silt, algae, and mild odor from decaying organics at the bottom. Well water is cleaner but may still have dissolved solids. Both require treatment before use.
🤲
Stage 2 · Lifting
Easy Hand / Treadle Pump
Water is lifted manually using a hand pump or treadle pump (foot-operated). Rotated among SHG women — just 10–15 minutes per day per person. No electricity required. Simple enough for any adult to operate independently.
🕸️
Stage 3 · Pre-filter
Coarse Mesh Screen
A simple wire mesh or cloth screen removes large debris — leaves, insects, sticks, sand particles, and floating matter. Rinsed with water once a week in 2 minutes. No replacement needed for years. This protects downstream stages from clogging.
🪣
Stage 4 · Settling
Sedimentation Tank
Water sits quietly in a covered tank for several hours. Gravity pulls remaining fine silt and suspended particles to the bottom. The clearer upper water is drawn off for the next stage. Reduces turbidity and the load on the bio-filter significantly. Can optionally add Moringa seed powder as a natural coagulant to speed settling.
🌬️
Stage 5 · Oxygenation
Enhanced Aeration
Water falls in a cascade over stones or through PVC pipes, splashing and spreading thin. This exposes the water to open air — dissolving oxygen and releasing volatile compounds (gases that cause bad smell). More oxygen also supercharges the biological activity in the next stage. Use a longer cascade or multiple small drops for best results.
🏺
Stage 6 · Core Treatment ★
Enhanced Bio-Sand / Slow Sand Filter
The heart of the system. A large tank filled with 60–80 cm of fine sand (deeper than standard 50 cm). Water trickles slowly through, spending 12–24 hours if possible. Two mechanisms work together:

Biological layer (schmutzdecke) — a living film of beneficial microorganisms on the sand surface that eats pathogens, breaks down ammonia (1.13 mg/L in source water), digests organic compounds causing taste and smell.

Sand bed filtration — physically traps fine particles, bacteria, and protozoa as water passes through pores between grains.

Every 1–3 months, women scrape just the top 1–2 cm of sand with a plate (15–20 min). The biological layer regrows within 1–2 weeks.
🥥
Stage 7 · Natural Polish
Local Natural Polishing Layer
Instead of commercial activated carbon (GAC), a thin layer of locally sourced material acts as a final adsorbent for any remaining color, odor, or trace organics:

• Burnt coconut shell charcoal — from village kitchens or local mills. Free or very cheap. Mild adsorption of odor compounds.
• Rice husk ash — abundant in Tamil Nadu. Natural silica-based adsorbent.
• Wood ash — raises pH slightly (good for hard acidic water) and adsorbs some organics.

Replaced by just pouring new material once a year. No trips to Madurai. No supply chain.
🧪
Stage 8 · Disinfection
Low-Dose Chlorination
One small measured spoon of chlorine powder added to the filtered water weekly. Kills any remaining bacteria and viruses, and provides a residual protection that keeps stored water safe. Low dose — safe for drinking, no unpleasant bleach taste at this level. By the time water reaches taps, bio-filter has done most of the work, so very little chlorine is needed.
🪨
Stage 9 · Remineralization
Calcite / Dolomite Remineralization → Covered Storage → Village Taps
A small amount of crushed calcite or dolomite stone added once per year to re-add calcium and magnesium minerals. This balances the water (prevents it being too soft or acidic after filtration), improves taste, and protects pipes from corrosion. Water then flows into a large covered storage tank, protecting it from recontamination. From storage, it reaches village taps by gravity or light pump.
💡 If mild odor persists in peak summer: Villagers can do a final boil for drinking water only — a common and trusted practice already known in the village. The filtration system removes the vast majority of contaminants; boiling is a simple backup for the hottest months when algae peaks in the pond.
Bio-Sand Filter — In Depth

The biosand/slow sand filter does the heavy lifting. Here's how each layer contributes.

🦠

Schmutzdecke (Biological Skin)

Top 1–5 cm. A living mat of beneficial bacteria, algae, protozoa. Attacks and kills pathogens, breaks down ammonia, consumes organic molecules causing taste/smell. Needs 2–4 weeks to mature after each scraping.

🏔️

Fine Sand Layer (60–80 cm)

Deeper than standard. Physical filtration traps fine particles, bacteria, cysts. Longer depth = more contact time = better treatment. Key upgrade in this system vs a standard biosand filter.

⏱️

Residence Time

Water rests 12–24 hours between batches when possible. More time = more biological action = better ammonia removal and organic breakdown. Pause time is free — just planning is required.

🔧

Maintenance: Scraping

Every 1–3 months when flow slows: scrape the top 1–2 cm of clogged sand with a plate, discard it, and rinse the surface. Takes 15–20 minutes. The biological layer regrows in 1–2 weeks. No tools, no chemicals, no skill needed.

🏗️

Construction

Can be built using local bricks, cement, and river sand — all available near Thanichiyam. Larger size than standard = slightly more cost but much better performance for pond water with moderate organics.
