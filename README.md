**New Alternative Approach: No Activated Carbon at All**

Here is a **completely different, even simpler approach** without any activated carbon (GAC). This removes the need to buy, store, or replace carbon bags every 1–2 years — perfect for villagers managing everything themselves.

### Core Idea of the New Approach
We rely more heavily on **natural biological processes** and **longer contact time** in the **Bio-Sand / Slow Sand Filter** (already the heart of the system). Research on biosand and slow sand filters in rural India and similar pond water shows they already reduce color, odor, and organics quite well through the biological layer (schmutzdecke) and sand bed — often making the water taste and smell acceptable without extra adsorption steps.

For your pond water (TDS ~737 mg/L, ammonia 1.13 mg/L, moderate organics likely causing mild odor/color), we enhance the bio-filter and add simple natural polishing instead of carbon.

### Revised System (Hybrid: Oorani Pond + Small Well)

**Flow:**
Oorani Pond (monsoon, large storage) **or** Small Well (dry 10 months) → Easy Hand/Treadle Pump (lift) → Coarse Mesh Screen → Sedimentation Tank → Enhanced Aeration → **Larger / Longer-Contact Bio-Sand Filter** → Low-dose Chlorination → Calcite/Dolomite Remineralization → Large Covered Storage Tank → Village Taps

**Key Changes (No Carbon):**
1. **Enhanced Bio-Sand / Slow Sand Filter** (main upgrade):
   - Make the filter **larger** or with **deeper sand layer** (60–80 cm instead of standard 50 cm) and **longer pause time** (water rests 12–24 hours between batches if possible).
   - This gives more time for the biological layer to break down organics, ammonia, and compounds causing smell/taste.
   - Studies show good reduction in color, odor, and organics with proper residence time — many rural users report the water tastes "normal" and acceptable.

2. **Better Aeration**:
   - Use a longer cascade or multiple small drops with stones/PVC pipes. More oxygen helps biological activity in the filter and naturally reduces some volatile organics causing odor.

3. **Natural Polishing Step** (instead of carbon):
   - Add a **simple layer of locally available materials** at the end of the bio-sand filter or in a small separate drum:
     - Crushed **coconut shell charcoal** (not activated — just burnt coconut shells from village kitchens or local mills — very cheap or free).
     - Or **rice husk ash** / **wood ash** (available locally).
     - Or a thin layer of **local clay** or **Moringa seed powder** (natural coagulant + mild adsorbent).
   - These are low-cost, locally sourced, and replacement is extremely simple (pour new material once a year).

4. **Pond & Well Management** (very important for odor control):
   - Annual desilting of Oorani to reduce decaying organics at the bottom (main source of bad smell).
   - Encourage simple floating plants or shade to reduce algae/organics in the pond.

**All Maintenance Tasks Remain Tiny (Only SHG Women):**

No heavy work. No buying special carbon from Madurai. Everything uses village or nearby local materials.

**Estimated Cost**: Still around ₹90,000 – ₹1,15,000 (slightly lower because no proper GAC drum needed; bigger bio-sand tank instead).

**Why This Works for Thanichiyam (2-month rain, 10-month dry):**
- Hybrid sources give reliability.
- Big storage tank bridges long dry periods.
- Enhanced bio-filter + better pond cleaning reduces organics naturally.
- Final water should have acceptable taste/odor for drinking (many similar rural pond systems in India work this way).
- If mild odor remains in peak summer, villagers can do final boiling for drinking water only (common practice).

This is **much simpler** for long-term village management — fewer things to buy or replace.

**Advantages over previous version**:
- Zero dependence on external activated carbon supply.
- Lower recurring cost.
- All materials ultra-local.
- Still very effective for ammonia (bio-filter strength) and basic organics.

If the water still has noticeable taste/odor after 1–2 months of operation, we can add a very small amount of local burnt coconut shell charcoal as polishing (cheaper and easier than commercial GAC).

Here is the **detailed step-by-step explanation** of the **No Activated Carbon** approach (the simpler version you liked). I have added what typically happens to each key parameter **after every major stage**, based on your raw pond water quality (pH 8, Hardness 120 mg/L, TDS 737 mg/L, Iron <0.3 mg/L, Fluoride 0.5 mg/L, Chloride 99 mg/L, Nitrate 13.91 mg/L, Ammonia 1.13 mg/L) and real-world performance of these low-cost rural methods.

Changes are **approximate and typical** (not exact laboratory values). They depend on design (e.g., filter size, flow rate, contact time) and operation. The system is designed for good ammonia removal while keeping everything simple and low-labour.

### Raw Water (from Oorani Pond or Small Well)
- **pH**: 8.0 (slightly alkaline)
- **Hardness**: 120 mg/L (low)
- **TDS**: 737 mg/L (moderate, permissible)
- **Iron**: <0.3 mg/L (already safe)
- **Fluoride**: 0.5 mg/L (safe)
- **Chloride**: 99 mg/L (low)
- **Nitrate**: 13.91 mg/L (safe)
- **Ammonia**: 1.13 mg/L (high – main problem, exceeds 0.5 mg/L limit)

### 1. Coarse Mesh Screen
Removes large debris, leaves, and floating particles.  
**What happens**: Almost no change in dissolved parameters. Slight reduction in turbidity if visible particles carry some adsorbed material.

- **pH**: No change (~8.0)
- **Hardness**: No change (~120 mg/L)
- **TDS**: No change (~737 mg/L)
- **Iron**: No significant change
- **Fluoride**: No change
- **Chloride**: No change
- **Nitrate**: No change
- **Ammonia**: No change (~1.13 mg/L)

### 2. Sedimentation Tank (Gravity settling, 1–2 days or more)
Suspended solids and some fine particles settle down. Organics attached to particles are also partially removed.

- **pH**: Minor or no change (~7.8–8.0)
- **Hardness**: No significant change (~120 mg/L)
- **TDS**: Slight decrease (5–15%) due to settling of some dissolved organics/particles (~650–700 mg/L)
- **Iron**: Slight reduction if any particulate iron is present (remains safe)
- **Fluoride**: No change
- **Chloride**: No change
- **Nitrate**: No significant change
- **Ammonia**: Minor reduction (0–10%) if some ammonia is particle-bound; still ~1.0–1.13 mg/L

### 3. Enhanced / Longer Cascade Aeration (multiple drops with stones/PVC)
Water mixes with air, increases dissolved oxygen, strips some volatile compounds, and supports later biological activity.

- **pH**: Slight increase or stabilization (often moves closer to 8.0–8.2) as CO₂ is released
- **Hardness**: No change
- **TDS**: Minor decrease (volatile organics or gases stripped)
- **Iron**: Can oxidize ferrous iron to ferric form (makes it easier to settle/filter later; remains <0.3 mg/L)
- **Fluoride**: No change
- **Chloride**: No change
- **Nitrate**: No change
- **Ammonia**: Partial removal/stripping (10–30% possible with good aeration, especially at pH ~8); brings ammonia down to ~0.8–1.0 mg/L. Also adds oxygen for the bio-filter to work better on remaining ammonia.

### 4. Larger / Longer-Contact Bio-Sand / Slow Sand Filter (main treatment stage)
Biological layer (schmutzdecke) on top + deep sand bed (60–80 cm) + slow flow + pause time. This is where most cleaning happens through biology and physical filtration.

- **pH**: Usually stable or slight decrease (7.5–8.0) due to biological activity (nitrification produces some acid)
- **Hardness**: Little or no change (~110–130 mg/L)
- **TDS**: Low to moderate reduction (10–30% possible, especially organics; final ~550–700 mg/L)
- **Iron**: Good reduction (oxidized and filtered; stays well below 0.3 mg/L)
- **Fluoride**: No significant change (remains ~0.5 mg/L)
- **Chloride**: Low reduction (10–40% in some studies)
- **Nitrate**: Little change or slight increase if ammonia is converted to nitrate during nitrification
- **Ammonia**: Strong biological removal (70–95% with good design, longer contact, and oxygen from aeration). Brings ammonia down to **0.1–0.5 mg/L** or lower – meets the limit.

**Odor and color** also improve significantly here through breakdown of organics.

### 5. Natural Polishing Layer (rice husk ash, burnt coconut shell charcoal, or local clay – thin layer)
Mild adsorption and additional biological/physical polishing using ultra-local materials.

- **pH**: Minor change or stabilization
- **Hardness**: Slight possible reduction if charcoal adsorbs some ions (usually negligible)
- **TDS**: Further mild reduction (especially residual organics; helps bring TDS closer to desirable range)
- **Iron**: Additional polishing if any residual
- **Fluoride**: Minor or no change
- **Chloride**: Mild reduction possible
- **Nitrate**: Little change
- **Ammonia**: Small additional reduction of any leftover ammonia/organics
- **Overall**: Noticeable improvement in taste, odor, and clarity. Makes water more acceptable without commercial carbon.

### 6. Low-Dose Chlorination (1–2 mg/L residual, weekly simple dosing)
Adds residual protection for storage and distribution. Kills any remaining pathogens.

- **pH**: Slight decrease possible (0.1–0.3 units) depending on dose and form used
- **Hardness**: No change
- **TDS**: Minor increase (from chlorine itself, usually negligible)
- **Iron**: Any remaining iron may oxidize and precipitate (filtered or settled)
- **Fluoride**: No change
- **Chloride**: Slight increase (from chlorine compound)
- **Nitrate**: No direct change
- **Ammonia**: Further reduction if any residual ammonia reacts with chlorine (forms chloramines, which are safer). Helps bring ammonia to safe levels.
- **Safety**: Provides protection against recontamination in the tank and pipes.

### 7. Calcite / Dolomite Remineralization Bed (passive contact with crushed local stones)
Water flows slowly through crushed limestone or dolomite. Dissolves some minerals.

- **pH**: Increases slightly (helps bring it to comfortable 7.5–8.5 range if it dropped earlier)
- **Hardness**: Increases (adds calcium and/or magnesium – improves taste and makes water less aggressive to pipes)
- **TDS**: Slight increase (due to added minerals, still well within permissible limits)
- **Iron/Fluoride/Chloride/Nitrate/Ammonia**: No significant negative change (all already treated)
- **Overall benefit**: Water tastes better (less “flat”), balanced minerals, and slightly more stable for storage.

### Final Treated Water (after Large Storage Tank)
- **pH**: ~7.5–8.5 (balanced)
- **Hardness**: 130–180 mg/L (pleasant, improved taste)
- **TDS**: ~550–750 mg/L (acceptable for drinking)
- **Iron**: <<0.3 mg/L (safe)
- **Fluoride**: ~0.5 mg/L (safe)
- **Chloride**: ~80–100 mg/L (low)
- **Nitrate**: ~10–15 mg/L (safe)
- **Ammonia**: <0.5 mg/L (safely reduced, usually 0.1–0.3 mg/L)

**Final Notes**:
- The **Bio-Sand Filter** does the heaviest lifting for ammonia and organics.
- **Aeration + longer contact time** in the enhanced filter is key for good results without carbon.
- If mild odor remains in peak summer, villagers can boil small quantities for drinking (common practice).
- All changes stay within or better than IS 10500:2012 limits for drinking water.
- The system stays very cheap, uses only local materials, and requires tiny maintenance (as listed in the original approach).
